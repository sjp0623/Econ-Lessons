Lesson 1 of 6
The Federal Reserve & Monetary Policy
Big idea: There's a secret button that can make borrowing money cheaper or more expensive — and 12 people in a room decide when to push it.

What is the Federal Reserve?

The Federal Reserve (the Fed) is the central bank of the United States. It's not a regular bank you can walk into. It's a government agency that controls the money supply and sets interest rates.

Think of it like this: if the whole US economy were a car, the Fed controls the gas pedal and the brakes — not the steering wheel (that's Congress).

Monetary Policy
How the Fed manages money supply and interest rates to influence the economy
Interest Rate
The cost of borrowing money — set by the Fed, affects every loan in America
Inflation
When prices rise over time — your dollar buys less than before
Money Supply
All the money circulating in the economy at a given time
🚄 CAHSR Connection

California High-Speed Rail has borrowed billions to build the train. When the Fed raises interest rates, those loans get more expensive — meaning more tax dollars go to paying interest instead of laying track. In 2023, when the Fed hiked rates to fight inflation, CAHSR had to recalculate its entire budget. The Fed never mentioned the train — but it changed everything.

Two main tools

Raise rates: Borrowing gets expensive → people spend less → economy slows → inflation drops. Use when the economy is "too hot."

Lower rates: Borrowing gets cheap → people spend more → economy grows. Use when the economy is sluggish or in recession.

Quick Check — pick the right answer
The economy is in a recession. Unemployment is high. What should the Fed do?
Discussion Question

If you were building CAHSR and the Fed just raised interest rates, what would you do — keep building, pause, or find a different lender?

Lesson 2 of 6
Fiscal Policy
Lessons 1 & 2 go together — the Fed (monetary) and Congress (fiscal) are two hands on the same economy. Same goal, different tools, different bosses.
Monetary (Fed)
  • Federal Reserve controls it
  • Uses interest rates
  • FOMC: 12 members (including the Chair)
  • Fast to implement
Fiscal (Congress)
  • President + Congress control it
  • Uses taxes & spending
  • Requires votes & bills
  • Slower — political process
Fiscal Policy
Gov. decisions about taxing and spending to influence the economy
Deficit Spending
Spending more than you collect in taxes — government borrows the rest
Stimulus
Government money injected to boost a slow economy (like COVID checks)
Austerity
Cutting government spending to reduce debt — opposite of stimulus
🚄 CAHSR — Fiscal Policy in Action

California's high-speed rail has received over $3.5 billion in federal grants — that's fiscal policy. Congress approved that spending. It's taxpayer money going toward infrastructure. Supporters say it creates jobs and grows the economy. Critics say it's wasteful deficit spending on a project that keeps missing deadlines.

⚖️ Gun Laws — Fiscal Policy Angle

California has spent hundreds of millions in state funding on gun violence prevention programs, buyback programs, and enforcement. That's fiscal policy at the state level. Some economists argue it reduces long-term costs of gun violence (ER bills, lost wages, incarceration). Others call it government overreach. Either way — it's a fiscal choice with an opportunity cost.

Quick Check
Congress passes a law giving $2 billion to expand CAHSR. What type of policy is this?
Big Picture — Lessons 1 & 2 Together

The Fed raises rates (slowing borrowing) while Congress is spending billions on a train. Do those goals conflict? What happens to CAHSR's loan costs?

Yes — this is called a "policy conflict." In 2022–2024, the Fed raised rates to fight inflation while Congress kept funding infrastructure. This made CAHSR's borrowing costs go up even as Congress added to its budget. The two arms of government were pulling in different directions at the same time.
Lesson 3 of 6
Supply & Demand
Big idea: Prices are not random. They're the result of how much of something exists and how badly people want it.

The core idea

Demand: How much people want to buy at a given price. As price goes up, demand usually goes down.

Supply: How much sellers can offer. As price goes up, sellers want to sell more.

Where both forces meet = the equilibrium price. That's what things actually cost in a free market.

⚖️ Gun Market & California Law

California's strict gun laws restrict legal supply — background checks, waiting periods, bans on certain models. When legal supply shrinks, prices tend to rise. Sometimes a black market forms where supply moves outside the law. This is a real economic consequence of regulation — not an argument for or against guns, just cause and effect.

🚄 CAHSR Ticket Pricing

Once built, CAHSR needs to set ticket prices. Too high → demand drops and the train runs half-empty. Too low → train is packed but loses money. Finding the right price = finding equilibrium. Airlines and Amtrak already compete for the same riders.

Lesson 4 of 6
Opportunity Cost & Decision Making
Big idea: Every time you choose something, you're giving up something else. The thing you gave up is the opportunity cost.

It's not just about money — it's about tradeoffs

Opportunity cost is the value of the next best option you didn't pick. If California spends $100 billion building a train, the opportunity cost is what that $100 billion could have built instead — schools, highways, housing.

Economists say: "There's no such thing as a free lunch." Every decision uses resources that could have gone somewhere else.

🚄 The CAHSR Opportunity Cost

California has spent over $12 billion on CAHSR so far — with a total projected cost of $100+ billion. That same $100 billion could fund roughly 200 new schools, thousands of miles of repaired highway, or years of housing assistance. Choosing the train means choosing NOT to do those things. Both choices might be good — but you literally cannot do both.

⚖️ Gun Law Enforcement Costs

California spends significant resources enforcing its gun laws — background check systems, law enforcement training, court costs for prosecutions. The opportunity cost: that same money and police time could go toward other crimes, mental health services, or schools. Even supporters of strict gun laws have to wrestle with this tradeoff.

Lesson 5 of 6
GDP — Gross Domestic Product
Big idea: GDP is the scorecard for the whole economy. But like any scorecard, it doesn't tell the whole story.

What is GDP?

GDP = the total value of all goods and services produced in a country in one year. It's how we know if an economy is growing or shrinking.

The formula: GDP = Consumer spending + Business investment + Government spending + (Exports − Imports)

GDP Growth
Economy expanding — usually means more jobs and higher wages
Recession
GDP shrinks for two quarters in a row — jobs start disappearing
GDP per Capita
GDP divided by population — measures average economic output per person
Real vs. Nominal
Real GDP adjusts for inflation so we can compare across years fairly
🚄 Does CAHSR Add to GDP?

Yes — construction spending adds directly to GDP through the "government spending" component. Every dollar spent on track, engineers, and steel = GDP going up. BUT — GDP doesn't tell us if that was the best use of those dollars. Critics note that GDP can go up even when building something wasteful. A bridge to nowhere raises GDP while it's being built.

⚖️ Gun Violence & GDP

Gun violence has a real GDP cost — ER care, lost worker productivity, incarceration, and reduced investment in high-crime areas. One study estimated the total annual cost of gun violence in the US at over $550 billion — nearly 2% of GDP. California's strict gun laws are partly an economic argument: less violence could keep workers productive and neighborhoods investable.

Lesson 6 of 6
Main Economic Philosophies
Every economic decision in this course comes back to one question: how much should the government be involved in the economy?

Capitalism / Free Market

Let private individuals and businesses make economic decisions. Prices are set by supply and demand. Government stays small. More freedom = more innovation, but also more inequality.

On CAHSR: Gov. shouldn't fund a train — let private companies build it (or not). On Gun Laws: Less regulation = freer market for firearms.

Keynesian Economics

Government should actively stabilize the economy — spend during recessions, pull back during booms. Named after economist John Maynard Keynes. Fiscal policy is central here.

On CAHSR: Government spending creates jobs and multiplies through the economy. On Gun Laws: Regulation is acceptable to reduce social and economic costs.

Socialism / Mixed Economy

Key industries (transportation, healthcare, energy) should be publicly owned or heavily regulated. Profit motive alone doesn't serve the public good. Most countries have mixed economies.

On CAHSR: Rail should be public and affordable — not a profit-making venture. On Gun Laws: Strong regulation is a public safety obligation.

Supply-Side / "Trickle Down"

Cut taxes — especially on businesses and the wealthy — so they invest and create jobs, which benefits everyone. Reagan-era policy. Critics say the benefits don't always trickle down.

On CAHSR: Tax cuts beat infrastructure spending. Private rail is more efficient than government projects. On Gun Laws: Less regulation = more economic freedom.
🏛️ California's Philosophy in Practice

California is often called the most Keynesian + Mixed Economy state in the US. It has the highest income taxes in the US, funds CAHSR through government spending, has the strictest gun regulations in the country, and has a massive public sector. It's a living laboratory for these debates — critics and supporters both point to CA as their proof.
